# Project

Throughout the course, you will deliver an application named "Concordia Urban Campus Safety & Navigation." Concordia is an "open" urban campus. In 2025, it saw record-breaking strikes (up to 27,000 students) and "unauthorized" protest actions. Students, especially those with disabilities or those feeling unsafe, need to navigate around blockades, vandalism, or "high-tension" zones in real-time. The system must help students (especially those with mobility issues or anxiety) avoid high-tension zones, blockades, or construction while maintaining a "Calm Tech" interface that minimizes panic during crises.

You are going to conceptualize, design, implement, and refine a system that will evolve from a paper description to a simple prototype and finally to a functional system.

What we will be focusing on in our evaluation of your work is your ability to:

- Evaluate or define some computing-related task or problem
- Design a solution to the task or problem
- Implement a prototype of your design

You are going to design your application based on the USER-CENTERED DESIGN. This means you need to consider all the course topics and materials in your project.

## Platform

There is no specific platform dedicated to this project. You can deliver your application for a phone, website, Windows Application, or anything that you rationalize to work on.

> **Hint:** Be careful when you choose your platform. Do not choose the platform based on your expertise, knowledge, or familiarity. Choose the platform based on your users' needs, tasks, and goals. See course material for details.

> **Hint:** It is expected that the design will change based on the platform you choose.

> **Note:** You are free to incorporate hardware components or re-use existing software, with proper acknowledgment (see week 1 slides and course outline). You do not have to buy any hardware or additional material for this course.

## Groups

Each project team should consist of 4-5 students, although exceptions may be made in consultation with the instructor. Each project group will be graded as a team, but students will be asked to anonymously rate for team members and this will vary students grade in the same team. If necessary, instructors will poll team members to check whether each member is making a proper contribution. Lack of participation by any individual may precipitate a grade reduction for that individual.

Within the team, you must negotiate on how much and what each person will contribute. Think carefully about your team members: Where do people live, and what hours do they work? What skills do the different individuals bring to the group? Students are not allowed to change their groups after the project pitch. The purpose of the project is to gain experience in applying concepts and techniques from the class to a real-world problem.

Remember: this is a course on human-computer interaction, not software development.

## Project Pitch (2.5%)

To pitch your team's idea, use the Moodle forum to describe your project (what are you planning to do, what problem are you trying to address, what are the user's goals, tasks, etc.). You will get feedback from the instructor and marker. This document is intended to help you quickly form project teams, decide on the specifics of your project topic, and begin preparation of your first formal deliverable, the "project progress report".

When you write your project pitch, consider the course material, and consider stakeholders, why a e.g., genius design is not appropriate for this project, user experience, etc.

You are encouraged to generate your own project idea. There are two important keystones to finding a good topic: 1) read a lot of papers in an area of interest, and 2) generate a lot of ideas. The report should be 4-5 pages and should include:

- A description of the problem you are addressing
- User goals and tasks. Requirements of your application.
- The reasoning behind why you think what you are proposing is important for users
- A description of your intended user population and modeling (e.g., designed personas)
- An explanation of how your system will fulfill usability components.
- A summary of the software, hardware, and other skills that you expect to need in team members to accomplish the project.

## Project Progress Report (2.5%)

For the project progress report, each group must submit a written report. Each report must follow a defined structure (see below). The reason that we enforce this structure is that the grading criteria will be based directly on the content that is defined by the structure.

At this stage, the user modeling and user profiles for your project must be completed. You need to report all the designs you had for this project.

Feel free to add to the structure when you have additional relevant, or interesting content. As with any written report, in addition to grading the document based on content, we will also be grading based on degree of professional preparation, expressiveness, grammatical soundness, and the ease with which it can be viewed and understood. A good design effort can easily be hampered by poor communication of what was done. Make sure that you produce a report that is illustrative of your efforts and process.

It is important to keep in mind that in user-centered design, you are not developing technology for yourself.

The key goal of this first substantive part of the project is to deeply understand the problem space that you are addressing, its set of pertinent users, and the issues and constraints that are involved in the problem. If other systems exist that address the problem you are studying, you should perform an evaluation of them. What are the shortcomings and challenges that continue to exist?

The most important goal here is to do research and identify important characteristics of the problem that will influence your design. A major mistake that students make on this part is to suggest potential solutions without first identifying the problem and its characteristics. Concentrate your efforts fully on developing an in-depth understanding of the problem at hand. Based on your research, you will start to get an idea of how you want to address the problem at hand and the methods you will use to do so.

The deliverable for this part is a proposal is a report with the first sections of a research paper. You are encouraged to use a template from an HCI journal or conference. The report should be 10-15 pages and have the following sections:

**Introduction:** What will you do? What are you trying to answer? What problem are you trying to solve, for who and why? State this as clearly as possible. What motivates you to explore this issue? What leads you to believe this is a problem/opportunity?

**User Tasks and Goals:** What tasks will users need to perform using the system? What are the primary goals that users aim to accomplish with this system? Who are the intended users, and what are their needs, preferences, and limitations? Why are these tasks and goals important to the users? What challenges might users face while performing these tasks, and how will your system address them?

**Methods:** Describe the development of the system. What tools (software/packages/hardware) did you use/will you use? What usability principles or guidelines will guide the design of tasks to ensure they are intuitive and efficient? What will be the main functionality of your system. You can add images of your system. Also, include your work on user modeling (e.g., designed personas, empathy maps, journey maps, use flow diagrams, user journeys, ...).

You are expected to implement a double-diamond design method (or similar methodology). It means, you are expected to design several designed personas, empathy maps, journey maps, use flow diagrams, user journeys, etc. You must include them all.

**Report on Workpackages:** Which student worked on which part of the project? A general assessment of group work. (Will not be graded at this point, but we will use it during peer-evaluation)

**Biggest Risk:** what's the riskiest component of your project? (may not be able to get a library you need, robustly implementing the \_\_\_, algorithm may take too long, the difference between conditions may not be measurable, ...) How do you plan on mitigating these risks?

## Project Report (5%)

The project final report and deliverables include a 2-3 mins teaser video describing your work and any implementations you have done (code on GitHub, webpage address, etc.), as well as a report of ~8 pages following the structure of a research paper.

Note you will draw on your proposal for the first part of the report so the more thorough the proposal the less work you will in the first sections of the final report. The report should have the following sections:

**Introduction:** Explain the challenges faced by Concordia students and why an application is needed. Highlight the primary goals, such as simplifying bookings, enhancing communication, and improving user engagement. Discuss why improving the user experience for students is valuable. Clarify what the application will and will not address.

**User Tasks and Goals:** Detail specific actions users can perform within the application, such as booking classes or tracking progress. Describe user goals, such as offline mode. Identify potential user difficulties (e.g., navigating complex interfaces) and how your design resolves them.

**Methods Design Approach:** Describe how the double-diamond design method (or similar methodology) was implemented. Include Personas, Empathy Maps, Journey Maps, User Flow Diagrams, etc. Include a description of low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes, visuals of designs, and mockups.

**System Functionality:** Core features (e.g., class booking, progress tracking). Tools, software, and hardware used (e.g., platforms like Android, libraries like React).

**Evaluation of Existing Systems:** Review similar security applications and their features. Highlight shortcomings (e.g., lack of user-friendliness) of your systems and opportunities for improvement.

**Challenges and Risks:** Discuss potential risks such as technical constraints, platform compatibility, or feature implementation delays. Explain how risks were addressed (e.g., contingency plans or alternative tools).

**Results and Discussion:** Describe the final prototype and how it meets user needs. Discuss findings from your evaluation of designs or prototypes. Highlight areas where the system could improve.

**Conclusion:** Recap the project's goals, achievements, and significance.

### Grading Scheme

**Writing /2**

- All sections are present.
- Details on the methods of your implementations are given.
- Details are given to determine the goal of the evaluation.

**Technical Merit (HCI/UC/UI) /2**

- A concise and detailed description of methods is given. Think about if someone could replicate the work given your Methods section.
- The evaluation addresses your research question, and the chosen methods are appropriate.
- The conclusion is logically tied to a range of information, including opposing viewpoints; related outcomes (consequences and implications) are identified clearly.

**Knowledge Gained /1**

It should be evident from the project that you have gained knowledge and understanding about HCI/UX/UI (e.g. principles, technologies, evaluations, frameworks, etc.) from the course. For example, you have demonstrated knowledge of the course content by integrating concepts into the report.
